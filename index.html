Human - AI Interaction in Modern Data Work

A guide to working with data, prompting AI, and scaling insight responsibly.

Introduction

The AI Data Strategist

As artificial intelligence becomes part of everyday work, a new kind of role is emerging. This role is known as the AI Data Strategist.

When an AI Data Strategist and a Copilot collaborate

  1. Goals are defined correctly
  2. Data is accurate, appropriate and aligned with real-world objectives
  3. Data-driven decision making is enhanced

Human Agency In The Age Of AI

Modern AI tools are often described as copilots.

A copilot:

  1. Suggests
  2. Responds
  3. Supports

The AI Data Strategist works with copilots.

Copilots rely on prompts. These prompts guide:

  1. What the AI focuses on
  2. How data is interpreted
  3. What kind of output is produced

Prompting as a AI Data Strategist's Main Skill

Prompting is about clear thinking expressed in language

An AI Data Strategist uses prompting to:

  1. Ask the right questions
  2. Frame problems correctly
  3. Define the context
  4. Set boundaries and expectations

For example:

  1. What data to consider
  2. What not to
  3. What does “success” mean in this situation?
  4. What risks must be avoided?

The quality of an AI’s output depends directly on the quality of human input.

  1. Humans provide intent, values, and judgment
  2. AI provides speed, pattern recognition, and scale

Prompts that define tasks, limits, and intent must reflect human priorities and support real world goals defined by people or else:

  1. AI may optimize the wrong outcomes
  2. Important context may be lost
  3. Decisions may appear objective while hiding bias

Chapter 1 — From Data to Information

What Is Data?

At a basic level, data is information about the real world. It is made up of individual pieces of information that describe things we observe, measure, or record.

Data can describe:

  1. People (such as names or roles)
  2. Objects (such as products or devices)
  3. Actions (such as buying, clicking, or sending)
  4. Events (such as meetings or messages)
  5. Decisions (such as approvals or choices)
  6. Outcomes (such as results or scores)

Examples of data include:

  1. A name entered into a form
  2. A temperature measured by a sensor
  3. A receipt from a purchase
  4. The time a message was sent
  5. A score from a test

By itself, data does not mean very much. It becomes useful only when it is organised, given context, and understood.

Context, Prompts, and Understanding Data

When prompts are tested and improved over time, systems become better at understanding the context of the data.

This helps them understand:

  1. What each piece of data represents
  2. How different data values relate to each other
  3. Which data matters most for a given task

As context improves, the value we get from the data increases. The same data can produce clearer and more useful results when the instructions are well written and focused.

What a Schema Does

A schema is a plan that describes how data is organised.

It explains:

  1. What fields exist
  2. What type of data each field contains
  3. How different pieces of data are connected

Schemas help turn semi-structured data into fully structured data. This makes the data easier to store, check, reuse, and understand.

How Schemas Improve Over Time

As data values are reviewed and studied, schemas can be improved step by step.

Looking at real data helps reveal:

  1. Common patterns in values
  2. Missing or unnecessary fields
  3. Inconsistent formats
  4. New or clearer relationships between data

This allows the schema to evolve by:

  1. Adjusting data types or rules
  2. Adding or removing fields
  3. Improving validation and consistency

Over time, schemas become more accurate representations of real-world information, better aligned with how the data is actually used, and easier to work with.

This leads to higher data quality and better understanding overall.

How Prompts Also Help Improve Schemas

Prompts do not just depend on schemas — they also help improve them.

As prompts are used repeatedly, they show:

  1. Which data fields are used most often
  2. Where information is missing or unclear
  3. Where the structure makes tasks harder than necessary
  4. How users naturally ask questions about the data

By reviewing prompt behaviour, designers can:

  1. Identify gaps in the schema
  2. Rename or clarify confusing fields
  3. Add structure where free-text is overused
  4. Simplify complex data layouts

In this way, prompts provide real-world feedback that helps schemas evolve alongside the data.

Schema and Prompt Evolution Together

Schemas and prompts improve each other over time.

  1. Better schemas lead to clearer, more effective prompts
  2. Better prompts reveal how schemas should be refined

Together, this creates a continuous improvement loop where data becomes more consistent, context becomes clearer, and information becomes easier to study.

As a result, data is well prepared for a study of data, including simple summaries, counts and totals, averages and trends, and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs).

At this stage, the focus is not advanced analysis, but understanding what the data is showing and ensuring it is reliable, structured, and meaningful.

When data, schemas, and prompts evolve together, they form a strong foundation for clear reporting, informed decisions, and future insight.

AI Data Strategist & AI Copilot – Raising Data Context Awareness

An AI Data Strategist focuses on how data is prepared, structured, reused, and improved over time. An AI Copilot uses this data to assist users by generating responses, insights, or actions.

Together, they work to raise data context awareness. This means the AI increasingly understands what the data is, why it matters, and how it should be used.

This process is iterative and improves through repeated use.

Profiling/Highlighting Values

A profile value is a known and trusted piece of information about a user, system, or situation.

Profile values can include preferences, roles, goals, common terms, or frequently used data fields.

Profile values help by:

  1. Providing starting context for prompts
  2. Reducing the need to repeat information
  3. Improving consistency across interactions

Well-defined profile values act as context anchors that guide how the AI interprets and responds to prompts.

Write Prompt

Writing a prompt means clearly expressing a request or instruction to the AI.

A good prompt:

  1. Clearly states the task or question
  2. Uses known profile values where possible
  3. Aligns with the existing data structure

At this stage, the goal is clarity rather than perfection.

Use Output

The output is the response generated by the AI based on the prompt and available context.

Using the output involves:

  1. Reviewing the response
  2. Checking accuracy and relevance
  3. Applying it to the intended task

The output provides immediate value and also acts as feedback for improvement.

Improve Prompt

After reviewing the output, the prompt can be improved.

This may include:

  1. Clarifying instructions
  2. Adding missing context
  3. Removing unnecessary detail
  4. Aligning wording more closely with the data schema

Improving prompts helps the AI better understand intent and deliver more reliable results.

Consider a Collection of Related Prompts

Prompts rarely exist in isolation. Over time, groups of related prompts emerge around similar tasks or data.

Considering prompts as a collection helps to:

  1. Identify common patterns
  2. Standardise wording and structure
  3. Reuse effective prompt designs

This shifts prompts from one-off requests into a reusable prompt framework.

Consider a Collection of Related Output Results

Outputs also form patterns when reviewed together.

Reviewing related outputs helps to:

  1. Identify inconsistencies
  2. Reveal gaps in data or structure
  3. Understand how the AI interprets similar requests

This step supports improvements to both prompts and schemas.

Improved Profiled Values

As patterns become clear due to organising prompts & outputted results after prompts are run, profiled values & the schema strucutre can be strengthened.

Evolved profiled values:

  1. Reduce repetition
  2. Improves accuracy
  3. Creates shared understanding across prompts and outputs

Over time, these values form a stable context layer that supports better reasoning and more predictable outcomes.

Raising Data Context Awareness Together

Raising data context awareness follows a continuous loop:

  1. Define and use profile values
  2. Write a prompt
  3. Use and review the output
  4. Improve the prompt
  5. Study collections of related prompts
  6. Study collections of related outputs
  7. Reuse and refine profiled values

Through this cycle, prompting and the data strategy improve together, resulting in clearer context, better-prepared data, and more meaningful outcomes over time.

Chapter 2 — Descriptive Statistics

What Are Descriptive Statistics?

Descriptive statistics are simple numerical measures that describe the basic features of profiled data values.

They help answer questions such as:

  1. How much?
  2. How often?
  3. What is typical?
  4. What is unusual?

Examples include:

  1. Counts (how many)
  2. Averages (typical values)
  3. Minimums and maximums
  4. Distributions (how values are spread)

These measures describe what happened. They do not explain why it happened or predict what will happen next.

Descriptive statistics do not tell why activity changed. They provide a clear picture of what occurred.

Human Agency Comes First

Descriptive statistics do not choose:

  1. What data is included
  2. What questions matter
  3. What success means

Humans make those choices.

Before an AI copilot is involved, someone must decide:

  1. Which entity is being measured
  2. Which attributes matter
  3. Which metrics are meaningful

Without this human framing, statistics become noise.

The Role of Copilots

AI copilots assist by:

  1. Calculating statistics quickly
  2. Organising large volumes of data
  3. Presenting summaries consistently

They are tools, not decision-makers.

An AI copilot can compute an average instantly, but it does not know:

  1. Whether that average is useful
  2. Whether it hides important variation
  3. Whether it aligns with human goals

That interpretation remains human.

Prompts for Descriptive Statistics

Prompts are how humans direct AI attention.

A prompt defines:

  1. What data to analyse
  2. What type of statistics to produce
  3. What level of detail is appropriate

Examples include:

  1. Summarise daily sales using averages and ranges
  2. Show distribution of outcomes, not predictions
  3. Exclude incomplete records

Each prompt is an act of human agency.

The AI responds only within the boundaries the human sets.

Humans Delegating AI

Equilibrium is reached when:

  1. Humans define purpose and meaning
  2. AI handles calculation and structure
  3. Humans interpret results and decide next steps

This balance prevents two risks:

  1. Humans being overwhelmed by raw data
  2. Humans deferring judgement to machines

Descriptive statistics sit at the centre of this equilibrium because they are:

  1. Simple enough to understand
  2. Powerful enough to inform
  3. Neutral enough to support many perspectives

Descriptive Statistics as a Shared Language

One key advantage of descriptive statistics is that they create a common reference point.

When humans and AI work together:

  1. Statistics become a shared baseline
  2. Decisions can be explained and challenged
  3. Assumptions are visible

This transparency supports trust and accountability, which are core elements of Human-AI interaction.

Why This Matters for AI-Driven Work

In AI-enabled organisations, descriptive statistics:

  1. Ground discussions in reality
  2. Prevent over-automation
  3. Allow humans to pause and reflect

They answer the question:

What is happening right now, according to the data we chose to collect?

Only after this question is answered should more advanced analytics or automation be considered.

Final Reflection: Staying Grounded in Data

Descriptive statistics are not old-fashioned or simplistic.

They are protective.

They make sure that:

  1. Humans remain informed
  2. AI remains assistive
  3. Decisions remain accountable

By combining:

  1. Human judgement
  2. Thoughtful prompting
  3. Supportive copilots

An AI Data Strategist and AI Copilot can responsibly continue working with data, while data context awareness evolves through continuous improvements to prompts and schemas, developed together over time.

Profiled Data Values Evolving with Prompts, Schemas, and Descriptive Statistics

Profiled data values are trusted, repeatedly used pieces of information that describe people, entities, processes, or outcomes.

These values are not static. They evolve over time as prompts improve, schemas mature, and descriptive statistics are applied to study the data.

At the centre of this evolution is a feedback loop between how data is structured, how it is questioned, and what the data reveals.

Role of Profiled Data Values

Profiled data values provide stable reference points.

Examples include identifiers, categories, status fields, timestamps, or commonly used metrics.

They help by:

  1. Reducing ambiguity
  2. Providing consistent meaning across prompts
  3. Supporting comparison over time

As these values are reused, they become increasingly reliable and meaningful.

Prompts as Drivers of Attention

Prompts determine how data is viewed and studied.

Each prompt directs attention toward specific values, attributes, or relationships.

Over time, repeated prompting reveals:

  1. Which data values are most frequently referenced
  2. Which values are unclear or incomplete
  3. Which attributes are essential for understanding outcomes

This insight feeds back into how profiled values are defined and refined.

Schema as the Structural Backbone

Schemas organise profiled data values into a clear structure.

They define:

  1. What data exists
  2. How values relate to each other
  3. Which rules apply to those values

As prompts and outputs are reviewed, schemas evolve to better reflect real usage.

Fields may be renamed, added, removed, or constrained to improve clarity and consistency.

This structural improvement makes profiled data values easier to study and reuse.

Descriptive Statistics as the Study Mechanism

Descriptive statistics are used to study the behaviour of profiled data values.

They do not predict outcomes or explain causes.

They help identify:

  1. Typical values such as averages or medians
  2. Frequency and volume such as counts and totals
  3. Ranges and variation such as minimums, maximums, and distributions

By applying descriptive statistics, patterns in profiled data values become visible.

This reveals which values are stable, which fluctuate, and which may need refinement.

From Profiled Values to KPIs

As profiled data values stabilise through repeated prompting, schema refinement, and ongoing descriptive study, they become suitable for defining Key Performance Indicators.

KPIs emerge when:

  1. The data value is consistently defined
  2. The schema supports reliable measurement
  3. Descriptive statistics show the value is meaningful and interpretable

KPIs are not imposed upfront. They are ascertained gradually as confidence in the data grows.

Continuous Evolution in Practice

This process operates as a continuous loop:

  1. Profiled data values provide initial context
  2. Prompts focus attention on specific values
  3. Schemas structure and stabilise those values
  4. Descriptive statistics study how the values behave
  5. Insights refine prompts, schemas, and profiles
  6. Mature values support KPIs

Through this loop, data context awareness increases and KPIs are grounded in real, well-understood data.

Summary

Profiled data values evolve alongside prompts and schemas through the study of data using descriptive statistics.

This ensures that KPIs are meaningful, reliable, and aligned with real-world behaviour.

The result is a balanced system where humans define meaning, AI supports structure and calculation, and data steadily becomes more trustworthy and useful.

What Are KPIs?

KPIs, or Key Performance Indicators, are specific measures used to understand how well something is performing.

They help people focus on what matters most by turning data into clear signals about progress, health, or success.

A KPI answers the question:

How are we doing, based on the data we chose to measure?

What Makes a KPI Key

Not every data point is a KPI.

A KPI is considered key because it:

  1. Reflects an important goal or outcome
  2. Is used regularly, not just once
  3. Helps guide decisions or actions

The difference between data, a statistic, and a KPI is intent.

KPIs are chosen deliberately.

What KPIs Are Made From

KPIs are built on top of profiled and structured data values.

They usually rely on:

  1. Counts, such as how many
  2. Rates or percentages
  3. Averages or typical values
  4. Trends measured over time

These are descriptive statistics.

They describe what is happening, not why it happened or what will happen next.

KPIs Do Not Exist Automatically

KPIs are not discovered by accident.

Humans must decide:

  1. What is being measured
  2. What good or bad looks like
  3. Why the measure matters

Without human judgement and clear definitions, a KPI is just another number.

KPIs and Context

A KPI only makes sense within context.

The same number can mean different things depending on:

  1. The organisation
  2. The goal
  3. The time period
  4. The audience

Context is what turns a metric into a meaningful KPI.

The Role of AI and Copilots

AI copilots do not decide KPIs.

They assist with them.

AI copilots help by:

  1. Calculating KPI values quickly
  2. Applying the same rules consistently
  3. Presenting results clearly
  4. Monitoring changes over time

The AI does not know whether a KPI is still relevant or whether it reflects real success.

That responsibility remains human.

KPIs Evolve Over Time

Good KPIs are not fixed forever.

As data improves through better schemas, clearer prompts, more reliable profiled values, and ongoing descriptive study, KPIs can be:

  1. Refined
  2. Replaced
  3. Split into multiple indicators
  4. Retired when no longer useful

This evolution is a sign of maturity, not failure.

Why KPIs Matter

KPIs matter because they:

  1. Focus attention
  2. Support alignment
  3. Enable comparison over time
  4. Encourage accountability

They help teams move from raw data to shared understanding.

In Simple Terms

KPIs can be thought of as a small set of agreed signals.

They are based on trusted data, interpreted by humans, and supported by AI.

When designed carefully, KPIs help organisations stay grounded, informed, and intentional in how they use data.

Chapter 3 — The Craft of Prompting: How Humans Shape Intelligence Through Language

If copilots are the tools of the new workplace, prompting is the craft that unlocks their power.

Prompting is a form of thinking.

It is the act of expressing intent clearly enough that another intelligence — human or artificial — can collaborate with you. It is the modern equivalent of interacting with a colleague, sketching an idea on a whiteboard, or outlining a plan in a notebook.What Prompting Is

Prompting is about:

  • clarifying your intent
  • exploring possibilities
  • refining ideas

A good prompt is a conversation starter.

Prompting Strengthens Human Thinking

When you prompt a copilot, you are required to:

  • articulate what you want
  • define the boundaries of the task
  • express your reasoning
  • clarify your priorities
  • reflect on your assumptions

This is why prompting preserves agency — it keeps the human involved

Prompting Is a Creative Act

It is expressive.

It is closer to:

  • writing
  • sketching
  • brainstorming
  • storytelling
  • designing

than it is to programming.

A prompt is a creative gesture — a way of shaping the work.

This is why prompting feels natural.

It taps into the same instincts humans have always used to communicate ideas.

Prompting Is How We Use Copilots

Prompts are for using AI.

Prompts are tools, and supported by human intent they are:

  • instructions
  • constraints
  • behaviours
  • styles
  • guardrails
  • personalities
  • workflows

The Three Levels of Prompting

Level 1: Task Prompting

“What do I want the copilot to do?”

Examples:

  1. Summarize this.
  2. Analyse this.
  3. Rewrite this.
  4. Model this.

Level 2: Process Prompting

“How do I want the copilot to think?”

Examples:

  1. Compare these options.
  2. Evaluate the risks.
  3. Identify patterns.
  4. Explain your reasoning.

Level 3: Identity Prompting

“How do I want the copilot to behave?”

Examples:

  1. Act like a strategist.
  2. Think like a data analyst.
  3. Write like a consultant.
  4. Explain like a teacher.

Prompting enables human agency to co-create with AI.

Prompting Is the New Literacy

In the new workplace:

  • writing is prompting
  • analysis is prompting
  • planning is prompting
  • modelling is prompting
  • creativity is prompting

Prompting is not a temporary skill. It is the new way humans shape intelligence.

Just as reading and writing defined the last century, prompting will define the next.

Prompting is the craft to definitively master for in order to pave the way for human agency and AI to evolve as a team in the modern workplace.
