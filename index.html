Human Agency & AI in Modern Data Work

A reflective and practical guide to understanding data, extracting meaning, and working responsibly with AI tools such as Microsoft 365 Copilot.

Introduction

Why this book exists and how to read it.

We are entering a phase of work where AI is no longer a specialist tool. It is embedded into everyday systems: documents, spreadsheets, chats, and dashboards. This shift changes not only how analysis is performed, but how responsibility and judgment must be exercised.

This eBook is not about learning formulas or tools in isolation. It is about learning how to think clearly about data in an AI‑assisted world— where the cost of producing analysis is low, but the cost of misinterpretation can be very high.

Chapter 1

Human Agency & the AI Data Strategist

Why direction, judgment, and responsibility matter more than execution.

Human agency in the age of AI

For much of the past, data analysis was defined by execution: writing queries, building models, and producing reports. Skill was measured by technical fluency and speed.

AI fundamentally changes this balance. When tools like Copilot can summarise, visualise, and draft analysis instantly, execution becomes abundant. Judgment becomes scarce.

Human agency is the ability to decide what is worth analysing, what assumptions are acceptable, and what conclusions should—or should not— be acted upon. This agency cannot be delegated to AI.

Key shift

As AI reduces effort, responsibility increases.

The emergence of the AI Data Strategist

The AI Data Strategist is not defined by mastery of tools, but by mastery of intent. They translate vague goals into analytical questions that AI can assist with—but not decide.

This role involves framing problems, defining success, evaluating trade‑offs, and communicating uncertainty. AI supports these tasks, but does not replace them.

Prompting as an expression of intent

Prompting is often misunderstood as “finding the right wording.” In practice, it is the act of expressing what you care about.

A strong prompt reflects:

  • The decision the analysis should support
  • The audience for the result
  • The assumptions that matter
  • The limits of confidence

Strategist prompt example

Analyse this dataset to understand
which factors most influence customer churn.

Constraints:
- Focus on long‑tenured customers
- Highlight data quality risks
- Separate insight from confidence

Output:
- Executive summary
- Key drivers with caveats

Exercises

  • Rewrite a vague prompt as a strategic one.
  • List three assumptions hidden in a KPI you use.
  • Identify one decision AI should never make alone.

Chapter 2

From Data to Information

How data becomes understandable, actionable, and meaningful.

What data actually is

Data is recorded observation: numbers, categories, timestamps, text, or events. On its own, data does nothing.

Meaning only emerges when humans decide what questions to ask and what decisions the data should inform.

Descriptive statistics as sense‑making tools

Descriptive statistics reduce complexity. They help humans answer a basic question: What is happening?

Counts, averages, medians, ranges, and distributions provide grounding and context—especially when validating AI output.

KPIs: choosing what deserves attention

KPIs are not neutral metrics. They encode priorities, values, and trade‑offs.

Choosing a KPI is a human act of focus: deciding what matters enough to be watched continuously.

Copilot, AI agents, and responsibility

Copilot can accelerate summarisation, comparison, and reporting. AI agents can monitor KPIs continuously.

But acceleration does not remove accountability. Humans remain responsible for interpretation and action.

Exercises

  • Define one KPI and explain why it matters.
  • List statistics you’d review before trusting AI insight.
  • Describe one context AI cannot see.
