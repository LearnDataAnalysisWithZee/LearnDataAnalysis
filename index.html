Human Agency & AI in Modern Data Work

A practical guide to extracting meaning from data while keeping humans in control— especially when working with AI tools like Microsoft 365 Copilot and agent-based automation.

Audience: Beginner → Intermediate • Format: Read on-site • Focus: Agency, clarity, decision-making

Introduction

Why this eBook exists, and how to use it effectively.

AI is becoming normal—not a specialist capability, but a default layer in everyday tools. That changes how analysis is produced, but it also changes what good analysis looks like. When output is easy to generate, the real challenge is deciding what to ask for, what to trust, and what to do next.

This eBook is designed to build a modern foundation. It focuses on three things: (1) how to keep human agency central, (2) how to turn data into information using simple, reliable methods, and (3) how to work with AI such as Copilot in a way that improves decisions rather than merely producing text.

How to read this

  • Skim the chapter summaries first to understand the map.
  • Do the exercises—this is where insight becomes skill.
  • Keep a “prompt journal”: what you asked, what you got, what you changed, and why.

Chapter 1

Human Agency & the AI Data Strategist

Why the most valuable skill in modern analysis isn’t producing outputs—it’s directing intelligence with purpose.

Human agency in the age of Copilot

Data analysis used to be defined largely by execution: extracting, cleaning, summarising, and reporting. Skill was often measured by how efficiently you could do those steps. As AI becomes embedded in tools like Microsoft 365 Copilot, many mechanical parts of analysis become faster and cheaper.

That doesn’t make humans irrelevant. It changes the nature of value. When execution becomes easier, judgment becomes the differentiator. The question shifts from “How fast can you compute?” to “How well can you decide what matters?”

That is what we mean by human agency: the human ability to choose the goal, define the question, set boundaries, interpret outcomes, and take responsibility for decisions influenced by data. AI can generate options and drafts; it cannot own consequences.

Key idea

Copilot doesn’t replace thinking—it shifts your work upward. You spend less effort on doing and more on directing.

Agency shows up in the questions AI won’t reliably ask on its own: “What decision are we trying to make?” “What assumptions are embedded here?” “What would be a technically correct but misleading conclusion?” “What context is missing from the dataset?”

Prompting Copilot like an AI Data Strategist

Many people start by treating Copilot as an answer machine: “Analyse this,” “Summarise that,” “Create a chart.” This can work, but often produces shallow output because the prompt contains no strategy. Copilot can only optimise toward what you ask for—and vague prompts produce vague results.

The AI Data Strategist uses Copilot differently: as a thinking partner that can explore, draft, compare, and iterate—while the human stays in control of intent. Prompting becomes less about “magic words” and more about structured reasoning.

A strategist-style prompt usually includes:

  • Outcome: what decision or action this analysis should inform
  • Scope: which segment, timeframe, or slice matters most
  • Constraints: assumptions, definitions, and what to ignore
  • Quality checks: limitations, missing data, or risks to call out
  • Format: how results should be delivered (bullets, table, narrative)

Example: modern prompting (copy & adapt)

Copilot, analyse this dataset to answer:
1) What are the strongest signals associated with churn for long-tenured customers?
2) Which patterns are actionable for retention strategy next quarter?

Constraints:
- Treat “churn” as customers inactive for 60+ days.
- Call out data quality risks (missing values, duplicates, inconsistent categories).
- Separate findings into: (a) insights, (b) evidence, (c) confidence level, (d) recommended next checks.

Output format:
- Executive summary (5 bullets)
- Then a short table of top drivers with brief explanation and caveats.

This is the core shift: we are not “only data analysts” anymore. Execution still matters, but the higher-value capability is strategy— turning messy questions into analysable problems, and turning AI output into decisions with clear reasoning.

Responsibility, limits, and trust

One of the biggest risks of AI-assisted analysis is false confidence. AI output can sound polished and authoritative—even when it’s wrong, incomplete, or based on shaky assumptions. This is precisely why human agency matters: it is the discipline of verification.

A useful rule is to treat AI output as a draft, not a truth. Ask: What evidence supports this? What definitions were assumed? What data might be missing? What would change my mind?

Responsibility stays with you

If an AI-generated insight influences a decision, the human is still accountable. Agency means checking logic, validating data, and stating uncertainty clearly.

Exercises

  • Take a vague prompt like “Analyse this data” and rewrite it into a strategist prompt that includes: outcome, scope, constraints, quality checks, and output format.
  • Write down one decision you care about (work, study, or personal). Then list: (a) what you need to decide, (b) what data could inform it, (c) what context the data won’t capture.
  • Identify one “dangerous” scenario where a polished AI answer could mislead you. Write two checks you would perform before acting on it.

Chapter takeaway: AI changes how analysis is produced. Human agency determines whether the analysis is useful—and safe.

Chapter 2

From Data to Information: KPIs & Descriptive Statistics

How raw observations become usable insight—and how AI accelerates (but does not replace) that process.

What do we mean by “data”?

At its simplest, data is recorded observation. It can be numbers in a spreadsheet, categories in a dropdown, timestamps in a log, text in a form, or events like clicks and purchases.

On its own, data has no meaning. A table of numbers does not tell a story. A list of transactions does not explain performance. Meaning emerges when humans ask questions.

This matters even more in an AI-enabled world: AI can process data, but humans decide what the data is for. If you don’t define purpose, you risk getting “analysis” that is technically impressive yet practically irrelevant.

Descriptive statistics: extracting information from data

Descriptive statistics answer one powerful question: “What is going on in this data?” They do not predict the future or explain causes; they summarise what is present.

Common descriptive statistics include:

  • Counts (how many?)
  • Totals and sums (how much?)
  • Averages and medians (what’s typical?)
  • Minimums and maximums (what are the extremes?)
  • Variation/spread (how consistent or noisy is it?)

These summaries help you see patterns you can’t see by staring at rows. They also provide a grounding layer: a way to sanity-check conclusions before you trust higher-level interpretation.

Why descriptive stats still matter with AI

AI can summarise instantly, but descriptive statistics help you validate: Is this trend real? Is it driven by outliers? Are we measuring the right thing?

KPI analysis: focusing attention on what matters

KPI analysis is often misunderstood as “tracking dashboard numbers.” In reality, KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) are decision lenses: selected measures that help humans judge whether something is moving in the right direction.

A KPI is not just a metric. It is a metric with intent. For example, “revenue” is a metric; “revenue growth rate” might be a KPI; “revenue growth among returning customers” might be a strategic KPI.

What turns a metric into a KPI is the question behind it: “What behaviour or outcome are we trying to understand or influence?”

KPI analysis typically involves: tracking changes over time, comparing to baselines or targets, segmenting performance (by region, product, customer type), and asking what intervention is warranted.

Agency moment

AI can calculate thousands of metrics quickly. Humans must decide which few deserve attention—and what “good” should mean.

Copilot and AI agents: acceleration with accountability

Tools like Microsoft 365 Copilot can accelerate KPI analysis and descriptive statistics by drafting summaries, comparing periods, highlighting anomalies, and generating narrative explanations. Used well, Copilot functions as a compression engine: it turns large data into digestible insight drafts.

In more advanced setups, AI agents (for example, built in Copilot Studio) can be designed to: monitor KPIs continuously, flag unusual changes, and prepare recurring “performance briefs.” These agents don’t create strategy—they operationalise it.

Every agent reflects human decisions: which KPIs it watches, what counts as unusual, how often it reports, and who receives the information. Agency doesn’t disappear—it becomes encoded.

Boundary

AI can observe and report. Humans interpret, decide, and remain accountable.

Exercises

  • Choose a process you care about (work, study, or personal). Define one KPI that would genuinely indicate success or failure—and explain why.
  • Take any dataset and list five descriptive statistics you want to see before trusting any AI-generated insight.
  • Imagine an AI agent monitoring a KPI. What would you not want it to decide without human review? Write two safeguards you would put in place.

Chapter takeaway: KPIs focus attention. Descriptive statistics provide grounding. AI accelerates insight—but humans decide what it means.

Chapter 3

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Structured Curiosity

A practical way to ask better questions, discover patterns, and avoid false confidence—especially with AI in the loop.

What EDA is (and isn’t)

Exploratory Data Analysis is the phase where you learn what your data is actually saying—before you commit to conclusions. It’s a discipline of curiosity: you look for distributions, relationships, anomalies, missingness, and contradictions.

EDA is not about proving a point. It’s about discovering what is plausibly true and what needs more evidence. In a world where AI can generate confident narratives quickly, EDA is one of the best protections against being misled.

EDA mindset

“Show me what’s there, what’s weird, what’s missing, and what might be misleading.”

A simple EDA workflow

A useful beginner workflow is:

  1. Orientation: What columns exist? What do they mean? What is the unit of analysis?
  2. Quality scan: Missing values, duplicates, inconsistent categories, impossible ranges.
  3. Univariate view: Distributions of key variables (typical values, spread, outliers).
  4. Segment comparisons: How metrics differ by group (region, product, cohort, etc.).
  5. Relationships: Correlations or patterns worth exploring (without assuming causation).
  6. Questions log: Document follow-ups: what you need to check next and why.

This is where descriptive statistics become practical: they let you quickly test whether a story is supported by the shape of the data. EDA often produces better questions than it produces final answers—and that’s a sign it’s working.

Using Copilot to support EDA (without outsourcing thinking)

Copilot can help you move faster through EDA by drafting summaries, suggesting checks, and helping you compare groups. The key is to keep the human in control: use Copilot to generate candidates, then verify using basic statistics and logic.

EDA prompt pattern

Copilot, help me do a first-pass EDA on this dataset.

Please provide:
- A quick data dictionary guess (what each column likely represents)
- Missing values and duplicates to watch for
- Basic descriptive statistics for key numeric columns
- Notable outliers or unusual distributions
- 5 questions I should ask next, based on what you see

Important:
- Clearly label uncertainties and assumptions
- Suggest sanity checks I can run to verify.

If you build an AI agent for EDA (for example, to run a recurring “data health + KPI brief”), keep it constrained: define what it monitors, what it can claim, and what it must flag for human review. Agents are powerful—but without guardrails, they can amplify noise into “insight.”

Exercises

  • Pick a dataset and write 10 questions you can ask without doing any modelling (only EDA).
  • Identify one variable that could mislead you if you ignore distribution and outliers—and explain why.
  • Create a simple “EDA checklist” you would run before trusting an AI-generated narrative.

Chapter takeaway: EDA is structured curiosity. AI can help you explore faster, but humans must keep control of meaning and claims.

Closing notes

A simple principle to keep.

The modern opportunity is not that AI can do analysis faster. The opportunity is that humans can spend more time on what only humans can do: define purpose, question assumptions, interpret responsibly, and decide wisely.

Final reminder

AI makes output cheaper. Human agency keeps decisions trustworthy.
