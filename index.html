Introduction

The AI Data Strategist

As artificial intelligence becomes part of everyday work, a new kind of role is emerging. This role is known as the AI Data Strategist.

When an AI Data Strategist and a Copilot collaborate

  1. Goals are defined correctly
  2. Data is accurate, appropriate and aligned with real-world objectives
  3. Data-driven decision making is enhanced

Human Agency In The Age Of AI

Modern AI tools are often described as copilots.

A copilot:

  1. Suggests
  2. Responds
  3. Supports

The AI Data Strategist works with copilots.

Copilots rely on prompts. These prompts guide:

  1. What the AI focuses on
  2. How data is interpreted
  3. What kind of output is produced

Prompting as a AI Data Strategist's Main Skill

Prompting is about clear thinking expressed in language

An AI Data Strategist uses prompting to:

  1. Ask the right questions
  2. Frame problems correctly
  3. Define the context
  4. Set boundaries and expectations

For example:

  1. What data to consider
  2. What not to
  3. What does “success” mean in this situation?
  4. What risks must be avoided?

The quality of an AI’s output depends directly on the quality of human input.

  1. Humans provide intent, values, and judgment
  2. AI provides speed, pattern recognition, and scale

Prompts that define tasks, limits, and intent must reflect human priorities and support real world goals defined by people or else:

  1. AI may optimize the wrong outcomes
  2. Important context may be lost
  3. Decisions may appear objective while hiding bias

Chapter 1 — From Data to Information

What Is Data?

At a basic level, data is information about the real world. It is made up of individual pieces of information that describe things we observe, measure, or record.

Data can describe:

  1. People (such as names or roles)
  2. Objects (such as products or devices)
  3. Actions (such as buying, clicking, or sending)
  4. Events (such as meetings or messages)
  5. Decisions (such as approvals or choices)
  6. Outcomes (such as results or scores)

Examples of data include:

  1. A name entered into a form
  2. A temperature measured by a sensor
  3. A receipt from a purchase
  4. The time a message was sent
  5. A score from a test

By itself, data does not mean very much. It becomes useful only when it is organised, given context, and understood.

Context, Prompts, and Understanding Data

When prompts are tested and improved over time, systems become better at understanding the context of the data.

This helps them understand:

  1. What each piece of data represents
  2. How different data values relate to each other
  3. Which data matters most for a given task

As context improves, the value we get from the data increases. The same data can produce clearer and more useful results when the instructions are well written and focused.

What a Schema Does

A schema is a plan that describes how data is organised.

It explains:

  1. What fields exist
  2. What type of data each field contains
  3. How different pieces of data are connected

Schemas help turn semi-structured data into fully structured data. This makes the data easier to store, check, reuse, and understand.

How Schemas Improve Over Time

As data values are reviewed and studied, schemas can be improved step by step.

Looking at real data helps reveal:

  1. Common patterns in values
  2. Missing or unnecessary fields
  3. Inconsistent formats
  4. New or clearer relationships between data

This allows the schema to evolve by:

  1. Adjusting data types or rules
  2. Adding or removing fields
  3. Improving validation and consistency

Over time, schemas become more accurate representations of real-world information, better aligned with how the data is actually used, and easier to work with.

This leads to higher data quality and better understanding overall.

How Prompts Also Help Improve Schemas

Prompts do not just depend on schemas — they also help improve them.

As prompts are used repeatedly, they show:

  1. Which data fields are used most often
  2. Where information is missing or unclear
  3. Where the structure makes tasks harder than necessary
  4. How users naturally ask questions about the data

By reviewing prompt behaviour, designers can:

  1. Identify gaps in the schema
  2. Rename or clarify confusing fields
  3. Add structure where free-text is overused
  4. Simplify complex data layouts

In this way, prompts provide real-world feedback that helps schemas evolve alongside the data.

Schema and Prompt Evolution Together

Schemas and prompts improve each other over time.

  1. Better schemas lead to clearer, more effective prompts
  2. Better prompts reveal how schemas should be refined

Together, this creates a continuous improvement loop where data becomes more consistent, context becomes clearer, and information becomes easier to study.

As a result, data is well prepared for a study of data, including simple summaries, counts and totals, averages and trends, and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs).

At this stage, the focus is not advanced analysis, but understanding what the data is showing and ensuring it is reliable, structured, and meaningful.

When data, schemas, and prompts evolve together, they form a strong foundation for clear reporting, informed decisions, and future insight.

AI Data Strategist & AI Copilot – Raising Data Context Awareness

An AI Data Strategist focuses on how data is prepared, structured, reused, and improved over time. An AI Copilot uses this data to assist users by generating responses, insights, or actions.

Together, they work to raise data context awareness. This means the AI increasingly understands what the data is, why it matters, and how it should be used.

This process is iterative and improves through repeated use.

Profile Value

A profile value is a known and trusted piece of information about a user, system, or situation.

Profile values can include preferences, roles, goals, common terms, or frequently used data fields.

Profile values help by:

  1. Providing starting context for prompts
  2. Reducing the need to repeat information
  3. Improving consistency across interactions

Well-defined profile values act as context anchors that guide how the AI interprets and responds to prompts.

Write Prompt

Writing a prompt means clearly expressing a request or instruction to the AI.

A good prompt:

  1. Clearly states the task or question
  2. Uses known profile values where possible
  3. Aligns with the existing data structure

At this stage, the goal is clarity rather than perfection.

Use Output

The output is the response generated by the AI based on the prompt and available context.

Using the output involves:

  1. Reviewing the response
  2. Checking accuracy and relevance
  3. Applying it to the intended task

The output provides immediate value and also acts as feedback for improvement.

Improve Prompt

After reviewing the output, the prompt can be improved.

This may include:

  1. Clarifying instructions
  2. Adding missing context
  3. Removing unnecessary detail
  4. Aligning wording more closely with the data schema

Improving prompts helps the AI better understand intent and deliver more reliable results.

Consider a Collection of Related Prompts

Prompts rarely exist in isolation. Over time, groups of related prompts emerge around similar tasks or data.

Considering prompts as a collection helps to:

  1. Identify common patterns
  2. Standardise wording and structure
  3. Reuse effective prompt designs

This shifts prompts from one-off requests into a reusable prompt framework.

Consider a Collection of Related Output Results

Outputs also form patterns when reviewed together.

Reviewing related outputs helps to:

  1. Identify inconsistencies
  2. Reveal gaps in data or structure
  3. Understand how the AI interprets similar requests

This step supports improvements to both prompts and schemas.

Re-use Profiled Value

As patterns become clear, profiled values can be reused and strengthened.

Reusing profiled values:

  1. Reduces repetition
  2. Improves accuracy
  3. Creates shared understanding across prompts and outputs

Over time, these values form a stable context layer that supports better reasoning and more predictable outcomes.

Raising Data Context Awareness Together

Raising data context awareness follows a continuous loop:

  1. Define and use profile values
  2. Write a prompt
  3. Use and review the output
  4. Improve the prompt
  5. Study collections of related prompts
  6. Study collections of related outputs
  7. Reuse and refine profiled values

Through this cycle, prompting and the data strategy improve together, resulting in clearer context, better-prepared data, and more meaningful outcomes over time.

Chapter 3 — The Craft of Prompting: How Humans Shape Intelligence Through Language

If copilots are the tools of the new workplace, prompting is the craft that unlocks their power.

Prompting is a form of thinking.

It is the act of expressing intent clearly enough that another intelligence — human or artificial — can collaborate with you. It is the modern equivalent of interacting with a colleague, sketching an idea on a whiteboard, or outlining a plan in a notebook.What Prompting Is

Prompting is about:

  • clarifying your intent
  • exploring possibilities
  • refining ideas

A good prompt is a conversation starter.

Prompting Strengthens Human Thinking

When you prompt a copilot, you are required to:

  • articulate what you want
  • define the boundaries of the task
  • express your reasoning
  • clarify your priorities
  • reflect on your assumptions

This is why prompting preserves agency — it keeps the human involved

Prompting Is a Creative Act

It is expressive.

It is closer to:

  • writing
  • sketching
  • brainstorming
  • storytelling
  • designing

than it is to programming.

A prompt is a creative gesture — a way of shaping the work.

This is why prompting feels natural.

It taps into the same instincts humans have always used to communicate ideas.

Prompting Is How We Use Copilots

Prompts are for using AI.

Prompts are tools, and supported by human intent they are:

  • instructions
  • constraints
  • behaviours
  • styles
  • guardrails
  • personalities
  • workflows

The Three Levels of Prompting

Level 1: Task Prompting

“What do I want the copilot to do?”

Examples:

  1. Summarize this.
  2. Analyse this.
  3. Rewrite this.
  4. Model this.

Level 2: Process Prompting

“How do I want the copilot to think?”

Examples:

  1. Compare these options.
  2. Evaluate the risks.
  3. Identify patterns.
  4. Explain your reasoning.

Level 3: Identity Prompting

“How do I want the copilot to behave?”

Examples:

  1. Act like a strategist.
  2. Think like a data analyst.
  3. Write like a consultant.
  4. Explain like a teacher.

Prompting enables human agency to co-create with AI.

Prompting Is the New Literacy

In the new workplace:

  • writing is prompting
  • analysis is prompting
  • planning is prompting
  • modelling is prompting
  • creativity is prompting

Prompting is not a temporary skill. It is the new way humans shape intelligence.

Just as reading and writing defined the last century, prompting will define the next.

Prompting is the craft to definitively master for in order to pave the way for human agency and AI to evolve as a team in the modern workplace.
