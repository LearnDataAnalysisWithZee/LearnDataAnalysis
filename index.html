Main Process

In conversation-focused customer service analysis, the starting point is the main process, because this defines the true purpose of the interaction from both the customer's and the business's perspective. In many service-led organisations, that main process is the quote. Customers are not calling simply to have a conversation; they are calling to understand pricing, suitability, availibility, and the steps required to begin the delivery of a product or service. By anchoring analysis around the quote process, conversation design remains outcome-driven rather than script-driven, therefore each interaction supports a measurable operational and commercial objective.

Although there is often far more involved than just producing a quote, in many businesses the quote is central to operations or acts as the point at which delivery of the product or service formally begins. For this reason it provides a logical and consistent focal point for conversation analysis. This is why the quote is intentionally selected as the main process within this framework.

This quote-led structure is common across a wide range of industries. Home improvement companies gather property and requirement details before quoting for windows, roofing, or renovations. Removal firms calculate quotes based on distance, volume, and timing. Despite variations in regulation, complexity, and product type, these businesses, and also alot more businesses, all share the same underlying requirement, structured information must be captured during an live interaction before a quote can be delivered.

“A conversation script can help with how I sound and what words I use, but during a live interaction, it doesn’t always matter how I sound or what words I used — what matters most is how much I help the customer. When every interaction prioritises helping the customer, it becomes an experience to remember, and memorable experiences build true customer service expertise.”

Schema

Because of this shared requirement, a common schema can be used to track and analyse quote performance across industries. The first table in this schema is the, Quote Register, that acts as the master record for each quote journey. Each entry represents a single quoting process and captures both operational and conversational context. Fields such as ID and Working Day allow the quote to be tracked over time, while Client, Postcode, and Contact anchor the quote to a specific customer. Product or Service Type and Product or Service describe what is being quoted, while Inception or Start Date records when coverage or service is expected to begin. Performance or Current Status reflects the outcome of the quote process, such as quoted, pending, declined, or converted. Agent or Handling Team identifies ownership, and Notes provide qualitative insight from the interaction, including objections, concerns, or special requirements raised during the conversation.

Running alongside this is the Quote Pricing Register, that focuses specifically on the pricing outcome of the interaction. This table links back to the quote register through the shared ID and client details, but isolates pricing events so they can be analysed independently. It records the Working Day the quote was delivered, who delivered the quote, the quoted price itself, and relevant pricing notes. This separation allows businesses to distinguish between the progression of the quote as a process and the delivery of price as an event, which is particularly important when pricing is delayed, revised or delivered through multiple channels.

These two tables provide a unified and cross-industry foundation for recording quote performance. The quote register captures the conversational context, while the pricing register captures the commercial outcome. This schema allows organisations to understand how conversations influence outcomes, where friction occurs, and how products or services perform once quoted. This is why starting with the quote as the main process creates a practical foundation for conversation-focused customer service analysis.

State Led Conversation Script Extended Using If Then Logic

As a result of the two core data tables - the quote register and the quote pricing register - raw operational data is captured in a consistent and structured way. These tables do more than record activity; they provide the foundation for actionable insight. By analysing how quotes progress, how prices are delivered, and how customers respond at different stages, organisations can move beyond reporting and into purposeful conversational design. The insight generated from this data is used to design conversation scripts that actively support live interactions while a quote is being delivered, assisting consistency, clarity, and improved customer experience.

These insights lead to the creation of a state-led conversation script, designed to guide agents or advisors through live interactions in a structured yet flexible manner. Rather than prescribing rigid wording, the script defines conversational states and uses IF/THEN logic to adapt dynamically based on context, customer behaviour, and previously captured data. This approach supports both over-the-phone quote conversations and face-to-face quote processes, such as in-branch consultations, home visits, or on-site assessments, where the same conversational principles apply.

The first state in this model is Direction and Introduction. This state establishes context and sets the tone for the interaction. The direction of the interaction determines how the conversation begins, whether it is an inbound call from a customer requesting a quote, wether will start the quote process there and then or schedule it for later, then an outbound call as a scheduled callback, a follow‑up to correct or confirm details, or a conversation initiated to accommodate a change in customer requirements. In face‑to‑face settings, this state may involve confirming the purpose of a meeting, referencing a prior enquiry, or acknowledging previous discussions. By explicitly defining this state, the conversation starts with clarity, professionalism, and relevance, reducing friction and uncertainty from the outset.

The second state focuses on the conversation around Questions derived from the data source schema, most commonly a fact find. This is where structured information required for pricing and eligibility is captured. While the underlying questions are formal and often fixed, the conversation surrounding them is designed to be natural, explanatory, and customer‑focused. The script supports transitions between topics, explains why information is required, and adapts as did with the variations in introductions, using IF/THEN logic when certain answers trigger additional questions or alternative paths. This state applies equally to phone and face‑to‑face interactions, so that data is gathered accurately while maintaining rapport and trust.

The final state is What Happens Next, which brings the interaction to a clear and confident close. In this state, the agent or adviser explains the outcome of the conversation and sets expectations for next steps. This may include advising that a quote will be provided later, outlining a review or approval process, arranging a follow‑up call or meeting, or discussing whether additional products or services may be relevant. The customer is also given the opportunity to ask questions, so they leave the interaction informed and reassured. In face‑to‑face scenarios, this may also involve confirming documentation, timelines, or next visits, while on the phone it may include confirmation emails or scheduled callbacks.

By linking structured operational data to these three conversational states, organisations create a closed loop between data capture, performance insight, and live customer interaction. The quote register and pricing register provide visibility into what is happening, while the state‑led conversation script determines how interactions ought to adapt in real time. This is so that both phone‑based and face‑to‑face quote processes are consistent, measurable, and continuously improved through data‑driven insight rather than static scripts.

DIA Exemption – Low Risk

All outputs within this ecosystem are classified under a DIA Exemption – Low Risk profile.

This means:

  • knowledge artefacts are approved, governed, and auditable

The low‑risk designation enables broader Copilot usage while maintaining trust and accountability.
